**“Brand-new ideas”**

In the history of science, many breakthroughs look like they came out of nowhere.
But when you zoom out, they’re often new combinations of facts that were already on the table.

A few examples:

• Einstein’s theory of relativity – Maxwell’s equations, the Michelson–Morley experiment, and Lorentz transformations were already known. Einstein did something different: he treated them as describing the real structure of space and time.

• Continental drift – Wegener noticed that continents fit together like puzzle pieces and that identical fossils appear on both sides of the ocean. The data was there; the interpretation wasn’t.

• The structure of DNA – when Watson and Crick proposed the double helix in 1953, it looked like a revolutionary insight. But the key ingredients already existed: Rosalind Franklin’s X-ray diffraction images, the chemistry of nucleotides, and Chargaff’s rules about base pairing. The breakthrough was realizing how those pieces fit together.

In each case, the facts were already known.
The breakthrough came from a new way of connecting them.

Which raises an interesting question in the age of AI:

Is creativity — human or artificial — mostly about finding new relationships in what we already know?
