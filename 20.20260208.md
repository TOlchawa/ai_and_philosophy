**My Lucky Prompt**

Based on arXiv research from the past two or three years, one thing is becoming hard to ignore:
in image generation, short, “lucky” prompts often outperform carefully over-specified ones.

Why does this happen?
Diffusion models don’t follow instructions step by step.
Text gets compressed into a limited semantic representation.
When a prompt becomes too detailed, attention gets spread thin, features start to clash, and the model drifts back toward high-probability patterns from its training distribution.

The research consistently shows:

attention dilution as prompts get longer,

parts of the prompt being silently ignored,

quality drops with excessive compositional detail,

higher perceived quality from short, semantically clean prompts.


The practical takeaway is simple, if a bit uncomfortable:


👉 Keep your “lucky prompts.”

👉 Better yet, save the good images themselves.


Why?
Because this exact result may never happen again.
Tokenizers change.
Attention weighting changes.
Alignment changes.
Training data changes.

Tomorrow, the same prompt can produce something completely different.

This isn’t magic.
It’s an unstable equilibrium in a statistical system.

If something “just worked,” archive the result, not the instruction.
Instructions are fragile. Outputs are real.
