**Measuring the Unseen**

Core approaches:

• Historical backtesting – Train the system on literature up to year T and see if it proposes relationships later confirmed by research.
• Expert evaluation – Domain experts review generated hypotheses and assess plausibility and novelty.
• In-silico validation – Run simulations (chemistry, biology, physics) to filter obviously invalid hypotheses before real experiments.
• Predictive power – A good hypothesis should imply observable consequences that future data can confirm.
• Ranking metrics – Evaluate how many useful ideas appear in the top results (e.g., precision@k).

Key risk: metric gaming.
If the model optimizes only for measurable scores, it may produce vague or trivial hypotheses that are technically correct but scientifically useless. This is a classic Goodhart’s Law problem: when a metric becomes the target, it stops being a good metric.

But the deeper issue is even harder.

When we explore genuinely new ideas, the objective function itself is unknown.
We cannot reward the system for reaching a desired outcome, because we do not yet know what that outcome should look like. By definition, a truly novel discovery has no predefined success metric.

In that sense, the problem is not just evaluation.
It is the absence of a clear target.

In practice, AI hypothesis systems must therefore combine automated evaluation with human expert review and long-term empirical validation.
